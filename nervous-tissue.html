Part 1

Organization of the Nervous System

The two divisions, the motor sub-divisions, and the three functions every task runs through.

Part 1 · Overview

The two main divisions

Nervous

The nervous system is the body's fast control system: it lets us feel, think, and act. It has two main divisions.

  • Central nervous system (CNS): the brain (in the cranial cavity, about 85 billion neurons) and the spinal cord (in the vertebral canal, linking brain to body). It processes information and issues commands.
  • Peripheral nervous system (PNS): every neural structure outside the brain and cord. It includes the nerves (bundles of axons; 12 pairs of cranial nerves and 31 pairs of spinal nerves), the ganglia (small masses of neuron cell bodies outside the CNS), and the sensory receptors that monitor changes inside and outside the body.

Part 1 · Three functions

The three functions

Nervous

Whatever the task, the nervous system handles it in the same three steps: a stimulus comes in, the information is processed, and a response goes out.

  • Sensory function: sensory receptors detect a change inside or outside the body and send the input toward the CNS.
  • Integrative function: the CNS analyzes the sensory input, stores it, and decides on a response.
  • Motor function: the CNS sends commands out to the effectors (the muscles and glands), which carry out the response.

Part 2 · The neuron

The neuron: cell body and processes

Nervous

A neuron is the signaling cell: excitable, able to carry an impulse, and unable to divide. Every neuron has three basic parts.

  • Cell body (the soma or perikaryon): holds the nucleus and cytoplasm; the metabolic center of the neuron.
  • Dendrites: the branched, tree-like processes that receive incoming signals.
  • Axon: the single long process that carries the nerve impulse away toward other cells.

Part 2 · The synapse

The synapse

Nervous

A synapse is the site where a neuron communicates with another neuron, a muscle cell, or a gland. At a chemical synapse there is no direct contact; the signal crosses as neurotransmitter molecules.

  • Synaptic end bulbs: the swellings at the axon terminal that hold the signal-sending machinery.
  • Synaptic vesicles: the tiny sacs inside the end bulbs that store neurotransmitter.
  • Synaptic cleft: the narrow gap the neurotransmitter diffuses across.
  • Neurotransmitters: the chemical messengers that carry the signal across the cleft to the next cell.

A chemical synapse. No direct contact; the signal crosses the cleft as neurotransmitter.

Part 3 · Neuroglia

Neuroglia of the CNS

Nervous

Neuroglia (glial cells) are the support cells of nervous tissue. They nourish, support, and protect neurons, make up about half the volume of the CNS, and unlike neurons they can still divide.

The four glia of the central nervous system
Glial cellRole
AstrocytesStar-shaped cells that give structural support, help form the blood-brain barrier, guide neuron growth, and maintain the chemical environment.
OligodendrocytesCells that form the myelin sheaths of the CNS.
MicrogliaSmall phagocytic cells that remove debris and microbes.
Ependymal cellsCells that line the ventricles and central canal and help produce and circulate cerebrospinal fluid.

Neuroglia of the PNS

Nervous
The two glia of the peripheral nervous system
Glial cellRole
Schwann cellsCells that form the myelin sheath around PNS axons and aid axon regeneration.
Satellite cellsCells that surround and support the cell bodies of PNS neurons in ganglia.
Two glia make myelin: oligodendrocytes in the CNS, Schwann cells in the PNS. That single difference decides whether a cut axon can regrow.

Part 3 · Myelination

Myelination

Nervous

Myelin is a fatty wrapping around an axon. It insulates the axon and makes the nerve impulse travel much faster.

  • Myelin sheath: a multilayered covering of lipid and protein wrapped around an axon.
  • Nodes of Ranvier: the gaps in the myelin sheath, spaced along the axon.
  • Saltatory conduction: the fast conduction in which the impulse jumps from node to node.
PNS versus CNS myelination
FeaturePNS myelinationCNS myelination
Cell that makes the myelinA Schwann cell, one per axon segmentAn oligodendrocyte, one cell for up to 15 axons
NeurolemmaPresent; it is the outer layer of the Schwann cellAbsent
Axon regenerationPossible; the neurolemma guides the axon as it regrowsVery limited, because there is no neurolemma
Myelination increases from birth to maturity, so an infant's nerve conduction is slower than an adult's.

Part 3 · Clinical

When the support cells fail

Clinical
  • Neurons cannot divide, so a tumor of nervous tissue almost never comes from neurons. It comes from the glia, which can still divide. A glioma is a tumor of glial cells and the most common type of brain tumor.
  • In multiple sclerosis, the immune system strips myelin from CNS axons and signals slow or fail.
  • The neurolemma on Schwann cells, absent on oligodendrocytes, is why a cut nerve in the arm can regrow but a cut tract in the cord usually cannot.
  • Because the axon depends on the cell body for proteins, an injury that separates the two kills the axon beyond the cut. The location of a nerve injury, near or far from the cell body, helps predict whether the neuron survives.

Part 4

Check Your Understanding

From recall to reasoning, then the takeaways and your study questions.

Part 4 · Recall (DOK 1)

Name the cell

Practice

Depth of Knowledge 1 · Recall

Which CNS glial cell makes the myelin sheath?

Answer

Oligodendrocyte. In the CNS oligodendrocytes myelinate (one cell for up to 15 axons); in the PNS Schwann cells do (one per axon segment).

Part 4 · Skill / concept (DOK 2)

Apply the term

Practice

Depth of Knowledge 2 · Skill / concept

An axon ending on the cell body of the next neuron forms which synapse?

Answer

Axosomatic. Synapses are named for what the axon ends on: a dendrite (axodendritic), a cell body / soma (axosomatic), or another axon (axoaxonic).

Part 4 · Strategic reasoning (DOK 3)

Reason it through

Practice

Depth of Knowledge 3 · Strategic reasoning

Why can a cut nerve in the arm often regrow, while a cut tract in the spinal cord usually cannot?

Answer

The neurolemma. The Schwann cell's neurolemma provides a regrowth channel in the PNS; CNS oligodendrocytes have none, so central tracts rarely recover.

Wrap-up

Key takeaways

Summary
  • Divisions: CNS (brain + cord) and PNS (nerves, ganglia, receptors); the PNS motor side is somatic, autonomic (sympathetic / parasympathetic), and enteric.
  • Three functions: sensory in, integrative in the CNS, motor out to effectors.
  • A neuron = cell body + dendrites + axon; it cannot divide. Know Nissl bodies, neurofibrils, microtubules, axon hillock, axoplasm/axolemma, axon terminals, lipofuscin.
  • The synapse: end bulbs, vesicles, cleft, neurotransmitters; named axodendritic / axosomatic / axoaxonic.
  • Classify by shape (multipolar, bipolar, unipolar; Purkinje, pyramidal) and by direction (sensory/afferent, motor/efferent, interneuron).
  • Glia divide and support: astrocytes, oligodendrocytes, microglia, ependymal (CNS); Schwann, satellite (PNS). Myelin gives saltatory conduction at the nodes of Ranvier.
  • Names: nucleus/ganglion for cell bodies, tract/nerve for axons; white = myelinated axons, gray = cell bodies.
