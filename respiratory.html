The Respiratory System

The respiratory system moves air in and out and brings it close to the blood. We follow it from the nose to the alveoli, grouping the parts two ways: upper and lower tract, and conducting and respiratory zone. The focus is structure. Dr. Sharilyn Rennie

Part 1

Plan of the Airway

Two ways to divide the system, then the upper tract and the larynx.

Part 1 · Overview

Two ways to divide the airway

Respiratory
  • Upper tract: nose, nasal cavity, paranasal sinuses, pharynx. Lower tract: larynx, trachea, bronchi, lungs.
  • Conducting zone: passages that only carry and condition air, from the nose to the terminal bronchioles.
  • Respiratory zone: where gas exchange happens, the respiratory bronchioles, alveolar ducts, and alveoli.

Part 1 · Upper tract & larynx

The upper tract and the larynx

Respiratory
  • The nose and nasal cavity warm, moisten, and filter incoming air; the pharynx (naso-, oro-, laryngopharynx) is the shared throat passage.
  • The larynx (voice box) connects the pharynx to the trachea. Its cartilages include the thyroid cartilage, the cricoid cartilage, and the epiglottis, which seals the airway during a swallow.
  • The vocal cords (folds) vibrate as air passes to make sound; the glottis is the opening between them.

Part 1 · Bronchial tree

The trachea and bronchial tree

Respiratory
  1. Trachea: the windpipe, held open by C-shaped cartilage rings.
  2. Carina: the internal ridge where the trachea forks.
  3. Main (primary) bronchi: one to each lung.
  4. Lobar (secondary) bronchi: one to each lobe.
  5. Segmental (tertiary) bronchi: one to each bronchopulmonary segment.
  6. Bronchioles: smallest conducting airways, smooth-muscle walls, no cartilage.
  7. Terminal bronchioles: the last conducting airways before gas exchange.

Lungs, Pleurae, and Alveoli

Where the air finally meets the blood, and what goes wrong.

Part 2 · Lungs

The lungs and pleurae

Respiratory
Right vs left lung
LungLobesFissuresNote
RightThree (superior, middle, inferior)Horizontal and obliqueThe larger lung
LeftTwo (superior, inferior)Oblique onlySmaller; a cardiac notch makes room for the heart
Each lung has an apex (above the clavicle), a base (on the diaphragm), and a hilum (where the bronchus and vessels enter). The parietal pleura lines the wall, the visceral pleura covers the lung, and a thin film of fluid fills the pleural cavity between them.

Part 2 · Alveoli

The alveolus, where gases cross

Respiratory
  • The alveoli are the tiny air sacs of the respiratory zone that give the lung an enormous surface for exchange.
  • Type I cells are simple squamous cells forming the thin wall; type II cells secrete surfactant that keeps the sacs from collapsing. (Cell types extend slightly beyond the notes.)
  • Each sac is wrapped in a capillary; the alveolar wall plus the capillary wall form the thin respiratory membrane that gases diffuse across.

Part 3

Check Your Understanding

From recall to reasoning, then the key takeaways.

Part 3 · Recall (DOK 1)

Name the zone

Practice

Depth of Knowledge 1 · Recall

Which structures make up the respiratory zone, where gas exchange occurs?

Answer

Respiratory bronchioles, alveolar ducts, and alveoli. Everything up to the terminal bronchioles is the conducting zone.

Part 3 · Skill / concept (DOK 2)

Apply the anatomy

Practice

Depth of Knowledge 2 · Skill / concept

An inhaled peanut most often lodges in the right lung. Why?

Answer

The right main bronchus is wider and more vertical, so gravity and airflow carry an object into the right lung.

Part 3 · Strategic reasoning (DOK 3)

Reason it through

Practice

Depth of Knowledge 3 · Strategic reasoning

A stab wound lets air into the pleural cavity and the lung collapses. Why does losing the seal collapse the lung?

Answer

The seal is lost. The sealed pleural cavity keeps the lung expanded against the chest wall; admitting air (pneumothorax) lets the elastic lung recoil and collapse.

Wrap-up

Key takeaways

Summary
  • Divide the airway into upper/lower tract and conducting/respiratory zone.
  • Air path: nose -> pharynx -> larynx -> trachea -> bronchi -> bronchioles -> alveoli.
  • Right lung = 3 lobes; left lung = 2 lobes with a cardiac notch.
  • The pleurae (parietal, visceral) and their sealed cavity let the lung glide and stay expanded.
  • Disorders hit the airways (asthma, COPD) or the exchange surface (emphysema, pneumonia) or the seal (pneumothorax).
