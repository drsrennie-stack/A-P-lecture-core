Anatomy of the Cell

The cell is the smallest living unit of the body. We name the three regions of a generalized cell, build the plasma membrane, meet every organelle, and trace a secreted protein through the endomembrane system.  Dr. Sharilyn Rennie

The Generalized Cell and Its Membrane

Part 1 · The cell

The generalized cell: three regions

Cell

A generalized cell is a composite model showing the features shared by most body cells. It has three regions:

  • Plasma membrane: the outer boundary that encloses the cell and separates it from its surroundings.
  • Cytoplasm: everything between the plasma membrane and the nucleus, the cytosol plus the organelles.
  • Nucleus: the control center, usually the largest structure in the cell.

Part 1 · Membrane

The plasma membrane

Cell

A selective barrier. The fluid mosaic model describes proteins drifting within a phospholipid sheet.

  • Phospholipid bilayer: the basic fabric, a continuous double sheet.
  • Integral proteins: firmly embedded, most spanning it as transmembrane channels, carriers, and receptors.
  • Peripheral proteins: attached loosely to one face; act as enzymes and brace cell shape.
  • Cholesterol: wedged among the phospholipids; stabilizes the membrane and keeps it fluid.
  • Glycocalyx: the fuzzy external coat of glycoproteins and glycolipids; the cell's identity badge for recognition and adhesion.

Fluid mosaic. Heads face the watery outside and inside; tails hide in the middle.

Part 3 · Form and function

Cells vary with their job

Cell

No cell looks like the generalized model. Structure follows job: a cell carries the organelles its task needs.

Three cells, three adaptations
Cell typeStructural adaptationWhy it fits the job
Muscle cellLong and packed with mitochondria.Contraction demands a constant, heavy supply of ATP.
Red blood cellLoses its nucleus and organelles.Frees interior space for oxygen-carrying contents.
White blood cellChanges shape freely.Can squeeze between cells to reach a site of infection.

Part 3 · Clinical

The organelle points to the disease

Clinical
  • Lysosomes that cannot break down their contents cause the lysosomal storage diseases, such as Tay-Sachs.
  • Cilia that do not form correctly cause primary ciliary dyskinesia, where the airways cannot clear mucus and infections build up.
  • Because mitochondria carry their own small set of DNA, mitochondrial disorders follow a distinctive inheritance pattern.
  • When you know what an organelle does, you can predict what fails when it is defective.

Part 4

Check Your Understanding

From recall to reasoning, then the takeaways and your study questions.

Part 4 · Recall (DOK 1)

Name the organelle

Practice

Depth of Knowledge 1 · Recall

Which organelle sorts, packages, and ships the cell's products?

Answer

The Golgi apparatus. The rough ER makes the protein; the Golgi sorts, modifies, and packages it for shipping.

Part 4 · Skill / concept (DOK 2)

Match structure to type

Practice

Depth of Knowledge 2 · Skill / concept

Which is a non-membranous organelle?

Answer

The ribosome. Ribosomes, the cytoskeleton, the centrosome, and centrioles have no membrane; lysosomes, Golgi, and ER all do.

Part 4 · Strategic reasoning (DOK 3)

Predict from structure

Practice

Depth of Knowledge 3 · Strategic reasoning

A cell built to secrete large amounts of protein would be richest in which pair?

Answer

Rough ER and Golgi. Protein is made on the rough ER and processed and packaged by the Golgi, so a secretory cell is packed with both. Structure follows job.

Wrap-up

Key takeaways

Summary
  • Three regions: plasma membrane, cytoplasm (cytosol + organelles), and nucleus.
  • The membrane is a phospholipid bilayer with integral and peripheral proteins, cholesterol, and a glycocalyx; surface specializations are microvilli, cilia, and the flagellum.
  • The nucleus holds the genetic code: envelope, pores, nucleolus, chromatin / chromosomes.
  • Organelles divide into membranous (ER, Golgi, lysosomes, peroxisomes, mitochondria, vesicles) and non-membranous (ribosomes, cytoskeleton, centrosome, centrioles).
  • The endomembrane route: rough ER → transport vesicle → Golgi → secretory vesicle → exocytosis.
