Part 1

Cartilage and Bone Shapes

The supportive connective tissues: cartilage first, then how we classify whole bones.

Part 1 · Cartilage

The cartilage tissue

Bone

Cartilage is a firm, flexible connective tissue. It has no blood vessels of its own, so it heals slowly.

  • Chondrocytes: the cartilage cells, each one sitting in a small space called a lacuna.
  • Lacunae: the cavities in the matrix that house the chondrocytes.
  • Matrix: a firm gel of ground substance with collagen, and in some types elastic fibers.
  • Perichondrium: the connective tissue sheath wrapped around most cartilage.

Part 1 · Cartilage

The three types of cartilage

Bone
Sorted by fiber and texture
TypeFibers and textureWhere it is found
Hyaline cartilageFine collagen, glassy and smooth; the most common type.Ends of long bones, the nose, the airways, the costal cartilages.
Elastic cartilageMany elastic fibers, flexible and springy.The external ear and the epiglottis.
FibrocartilageThick bundles of collagen, tough and shock-absorbing.The intervertebral discs, the knee menisci, the pubic symphysis.
Cartilage grows two ways: appositional (new cartilage added at the surface, from the perichondrium) and interstitial (it expands from within as chondrocytes divide inside the matrix).

Part 2 · Microanatomy

Compact bone: the osteon

Bone

The osteon (Haversian system) is the structural unit of compact bone: a set of tubes around a canal.

  • Central canal: the Haversian canal at the core, carrying blood vessels and nerves.
  • Perforating canal: the Volkmann canal that runs crosswise and links central canals.
  • Lamellae: the concentric rings of bony matrix around the central canal.
  • Lacunae: small cavities between the lamellae, each holding one osteocyte.
  • Canaliculi: tiny channels that connect lacunae so osteocytes exchange materials.
  • Interstitial lamellae: leftover fragments of old osteons between intact ones; circumferential lamellae wrap the whole outer and inner shaft.

One osteon. Compact bone is many of these packed along the lines of stress.

Bone Formation and Growth

The two routes that form bone, the two ways it grows, and the ordered zones of the growth plate.

Part 3 · Formation

Ossification: two routes

Bone
How bone first forms

The chemistry of ossification belongs to physiology. In anatomy we name the routes, the structures, and the sequence.

Part 3 · Growth

The two ways a long bone grows

Bone

The epiphyseal plate is the disc of hyaline cartilage where lengthening happens. When growth ends, the plate closes and leaves the epiphyseal line.

Two directions of growth
TypeDirectionWhere it happens
Interstitial growthThe bone grows longer.At the epiphyseal plate.
Appositional growthThe bone grows wider.At the outer surface, beneath the periosteum.

Part 3 · Growth plate

Zones of the growth plate

Bone

Five zones in order, from the epiphysis at the top to the diaphysis at the bottom:

  1. Zone of resting cartilage anchors the plate to the epiphysis.
  2. Zone of proliferation: chondrocytes divide and stack into columns.
  3. Zone of hypertrophy: the chondrocytes enlarge.
  4. Zone of calcification: the cartilage matrix calcifies.
  5. Zone of ossification: cartilage is replaced by bone next to the diaphysis.

Top to bottom. Cartilage at the epiphysis, bone at the diaphysis.

Part 3 · The big picture

The supportive connective tissues

Bone

Bone histology fits inside one family. Each branch has its own structural unit.

  • Supportive connective tissuesfirm matrix for support
    • Cartilagehyaline, elastic, fibrocartilage
    • Compact bonebuilt from osteons
    • Spongy bonetrabeculae, red marrow in the spaces

Part 3 · Clinical

Bone structure shows up in every X-ray

Clinical
  • The growth plate is cartilage, so on a child's X-ray it looks like a gap. A fracture that runs through the plate can slow or stop a bone from growing, which is why pediatric fractures are described by how they cross it.
  • The epiphyseal line is the closed plate. Its presence tells a clinician the skeleton is mature.
  • Compact bone is built from osteons aligned with the lines of stress, so a bone is strongest along its long axis. A twisting force, off that axis, is the one that tends to break it.

Part 4

Check Your Understanding

From recall to reasoning, then the takeaways and your study questions.

Part 4 · Recall (DOK 1)

Name the unit

Practice

Depth of Knowledge 1 · Recall

What is the structural unit of compact bone?

Answer

The osteon. It is the central canal with its concentric lamellae, lacunae, and canaliculi. Spongy bone has no osteons; it is built from trabeculae.

Part 4 · Skill / concept (DOK 2)

Apply the term

Practice

Depth of Knowledge 2 · Skill / concept

A long bone grows longer at which site?

Answer

The epiphyseal plate (interstitial growth, longer). Growth in width is appositional, beneath the periosteum.

Part 4 · Strategic reasoning (DOK 3)

Reason it through

Practice

Depth of Knowledge 3 · Strategic reasoning

Why does a twisting force tend to break a long bone, while a force straight down its length usually does not?

Answer

Osteon alignment. Compact bone is built from osteons packed along the lines of stress (the long axis), so the bone resists lengthwise load well but is vulnerable to a twisting force off that axis.

Wrap-up

Key takeaways

Summary
  • Cartilage = chondrocytes in lacunae within a matrix, wrapped by perichondrium; three types: hyaline, elastic, fibrocartilage.
  • Bones by shape: long, short, flat, irregular, sesamoid. A long bone runs diaphysis, epiphysis, metaphysis, medullary cavity, with periosteum outside and endosteum within.
  • Compact bone is built from osteons (central canal, lamellae, lacunae, canaliculi); spongy bone is trabeculae with red marrow and no osteons.
  • Four bone cells: osteogenic, osteoblast, osteocyte, osteoclast (the osteoclast comes from a blood-cell line).
  • Bone forms by intramembranous or endochondral ossification; it grows longer at the plate (interstitial) and wider at the surface (appositional) through five ordered zones.
