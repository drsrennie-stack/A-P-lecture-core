The Axial Skeleton

The skull, the vertebral column, and the thoracic cage, the central axis of the body. We name every bone, its key markings, the sutures and fontanelles, the foramina, a typical vertebra, and the sternum and ribs.  Dr. Sharilyn Rennie

Objectives

By the end

Axial
  • Distinguish the cranial bones from the facial bones and name the bones in each group.
  • Identify the major sutures, fontanelles, foramina, and the bones of the orbit, nasal cavity, and hard palate.
  • Name the regions and curvatures of the vertebral column and label a typical vertebra.
  • Distinguish cervical, thoracic, and lumbar vertebrae and describe the atlas, axis, sacrum, and coccyx.
  • Identify the bones and markings of the thoracic cage.

Part 1

The Skull

Twenty-two bones in two groups: the cranium that encloses the brain, and the facial skeleton that frames the face.

Part 1 · Overview

The skull, an overview

Skull
  • The skull is 22 bones, plus the associated hyoid bone of the neck (counted separately).
  • The cranium is the eight cranial bones that enclose and protect the brain; the facial skeleton is the fourteen facial bones that frame the face and hold the teeth.
  • The calvaria is the domed roof (skullcap); the cranial base is the floor, shaped into anterior, middle, and posterior cranial fossae.

Part 1 · Cavities

Cavities, sinuses, and the hyoid

Skull
  • The orbit: the bony eye socket, built from seven bones (frontal, sphenoid, zygomatic, maxilla, lacrimal, ethmoid, palatine).
  • Nasal cavity: the space behind the nose; the nasal septum is the vomer + perpendicular plate of the ethmoid + septal cartilage.
  • Hard palate: the bony roof of the mouth, maxillae in front and palatine bones behind.
  • Hyoid bone: a U-shaped bone in the neck that anchors the tongue; the only bone that articulates with no other bone.

Part 1 · Clinical

Where the skull is thin, and its one big door

Clinical
  • The pterion is the thinnest part of the skull, and the middle meningeal artery runs just beneath it. A blow there can tear the artery and bleed into the skull.
  • The paranasal sinuses drain into the nasal cavity, so a head cold can spread as sinusitis; the maxillary sinus sits so close to the upper teeth that a tooth infection can reach it.
  • A newborn is checked at the anterior fontanelle: sunken can signal dehydration, bulging can signal raised intracranial pressure.
  • The foramen magnum is the one large door between brain and cord; rising pressure can force brain tissue down through it, an emergency.

Part 2 · Curvatures

The four curvatures

Spine

Four front-to-back curves act like a spring. Primary curves are present at birth; secondary curves develop with infancy's milestones.

  • Cervical: secondary, develops as the infant holds up its head.
  • Thoracic: primary, present from birth.
  • Lumbar: secondary, develops as the child stands and walks.
  • Sacral: primary, present from birth.

A spring. Abnormal: scoliosis (sideways), kyphosis (thoracic), lordosis (lumbar).

Part 2 · Regions

Telling the regions apart

Spine
One feature identifies each
RegionBodyDistinctive feature
Cervical (C1–C7)Small, oval; spinous often bifid.Transverse foramina pass the vertebral arteries.
Thoracic (T1–T12)Heart-shaped; spinous long, angled down.Costal facets where the ribs articulate.
Lumbar (L1–L5)Thick, kidney-shaped, largest.Massive build; no costal facets, no transverse foramina.
Atlas (C1): a ring with no body, holds up the skull (the “yes” nod at the atlanto-occipital joint). Axis (C2): bears the dens, the peg the atlas pivots around for the “no” rotation. C7 is the vertebra prominens.

The Thoracic Cage

The sternum in front, the ribs on the sides, the thoracic vertebrae behind: a shield for the heart and lungs.

Part 3 · Clinical

A cord inside, a pump behind

Clinical
  • A herniated disc: the soft nucleus pulposus pushes through a torn anulus fibrosus and presses on a spinal nerve at an intervertebral foramen, so the pain travels down a limb.
  • The dens is held against the atlas by a ligament; a sharp blow can fracture it, a serious injury so close to the brainstem.
  • The sternal angle sits at the second rib, the start for counting ribs; the xiphoid process guides hand position for CPR.
  • Abnormal curves: scoliosis (sideways), kyphosis (thoracic), lordosis (lumbar).

Part 4

Check Your Understanding

From recall to reasoning, then the takeaways and your study questions.

Part 4 · Recall (DOK 1)

Name the structure

Practice

Depth of Knowledge 1 · Recall

Which large opening transmits the spinal cord?

Answer

The foramen magnum of the occipital bone. It also passes the vertebral arteries and the spinal accessory nerve (CN XI).

Part 4 · Skill / concept (DOK 2)

Identify the region

Practice

Depth of Knowledge 2 · Skill / concept

A vertebra with transverse foramina is from which region?

Answer

Cervical. Transverse foramina pass the vertebral arteries. Thoracic vertebrae have costal facets; lumbar are massive with neither.

Part 4 · Strategic reasoning (DOK 3)

Reason it through

Practice

Depth of Knowledge 3 · Strategic reasoning

Why does a herniated lumbar disc often cause pain that travels down the leg?

Answer

Nerve compression at the foramen. The soft nucleus pulposus pushes through the torn anulus fibrosus and presses the spinal nerve where it exits, so the pain follows that nerve down the limb.

Wrap-up

Key takeaways

Summary
  • The skull is 22 bones: 8 cranial (frontal, parietal ×2, temporal ×2, occipital, sphenoid, ethmoid) and 14 facial; the hyoid is counted separately.
  • Four sutures (coronal, sagittal, lambdoid, squamous) plus the thin pterion; four fontanelles in the infant.
  • The column is 26 bones in five regions, with four curvatures (two primary, two secondary).
  • A vertebra = body + arch (pedicles, laminae) around the vertebral foramen, with spinous, transverse, and articular processes; discs are anulus + nucleus pulposus.
  • The cage = sternum (manubrium, body, xiphoid) + ribs (true 1–7, false 8–12, floating 11–12); the sternal angle marks rib 2.

Given, not Googled

Study questions

Synthesis

Answer these in writing, in your own words. Bring them to class; they are the reasoning the exams ask for.

  • Name the eight cranial bones and contrast their job with that of the facial bones.
  • Trace the four major sutures and name the bones each one joins.
  • Name the five regions of the vertebral column and how many vertebrae are in each.
  • Explain how the ribs attach anteriorly, and how that distinguishes true, false, and floating ribs.
