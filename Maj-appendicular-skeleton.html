The Appendicular Skeleton

The limbs and the girdles that attach them to the axial skeleton. We name every bone of the upper and lower limb, its key markings, and the bone-marking vocabulary that repeats on all of them.  Dr. Sharilyn Rennie

Objectives

By the end

Appendicular
  • Name the bones of the pectoral girdle and the upper limb, and their key markings.
  • Name the three bones that fuse to form the hip bone, and the markings of the pelvis.
  • Identify the key markings of the humerus, radius, ulna, femur, patella, tibia, and fibula.
  • Name the bones of the hand (eight carpals in two rows) and the foot (seven tarsals and the arches).
  • Use the bone-marking vocabulary: projection, depression, opening.

Part 1 · Girdle

The pectoral girdle

Upper limb
Clavicle, scapula, and their joints
BoneKey markings
ClavicleThe S-shaped collarbone; sternal end (to the manubrium) and acromial end (to the acromion). The only bony link of the upper limb to the axial skeleton.
ScapulaThe triangular shoulder blade: superior/medial/lateral borders, the spine, the acromion (point of the shoulder), coracoid process, glenoid cavity (socket for the humerus), and the supraspinous, infraspinous, and subscapular fossae.
JointsSternoclavicular (the only limb-to-axial joint), acromioclavicular, and glenohumeral (the shoulder joint).

Part 1 · Arm

The humerus

Upper limb
The single bone of the arm, proximal to distal
RegionMarkings
ProximalHead (into the glenoid cavity), anatomical neck, surgical neck (common fracture), greater and lesser tubercles, intertubercular sulcus (a biceps tendon).
ShaftDeltoid tuberosity (deltoid attachment); the radial groove on the posterior shaft carries the radial nerve.
DistalCapitulum (to the radius), trochlea (to the ulna), medial and lateral epicondyles, olecranon fossa (receives the ulna when the elbow straightens).

Part 1 · Forearm

The radius and the ulna

Upper limb
Side of the forearm and markings at each end
BoneSideProximal & distal markings
RadiusLateral (thumb side)Disc-shaped head (to the capitulum), neck, radial tuberosity; distally a styloid process and ulnar notch.
UlnaMedial (little-finger side)Olecranon (point of the elbow), coronoid process, trochlear notch (grips the trochlea), radial notch; distally a styloid process.
Three radioulnar joints bind the two bones: proximal, distal, and the interosseous membrane (a syndesmosis) between the shafts. The ulnar nerve behind the medial epicondyle is the “funny bone.”

Part 1 · Hand

The hand

Upper limb
  • Carpals: eight wrist bones in two rows. Proximal: scaphoid, lunate, triquetrum, pisiform. Distal: trapezium, trapezoid, capitate, hamate. Memory aid: “So Long To Pinky, Here Comes The Thumb.”
  • Metacarpals: five bones of the palm, numbered 1 to 5 from the thumb.
  • Phalanges: fourteen per hand. The thumb (pollex) has two; each finger has three (proximal, middle, distal).
  • Carpal tunnel: the flexor retinaculum roofs the carpals; the long flexor tendons and the median nerve pass beneath. Narrowing causes carpal tunnel syndrome.

Part 1 · Clinical

When you fall on an outstretched hand

Clinical
  • The clavicle is the most commonly broken bone, usually in its middle third, taking the force of a fall onto the shoulder.
  • The surgical neck of the humerus fractures often, and the axillary nerve runs close by, so a break there is checked for nerve injury.
  • The scaphoid is the most often fractured carpal; its poor blood supply means a missed fracture may not heal. The clue is tenderness in the anatomical snuffbox.
  • The ulnar nerve behind the medial epicondyle is the funny bone; a knock sends a jolt to the little finger.

Part 2 · Girdle

The hip bone: three bones fuse

Lower limb

Each hip bone (os coxae) begins as three bones that fuse at the acetabulum.

  • Ilium: the fan-shaped superior part; iliac crest, anterior and posterior superior iliac spines, iliac fossa, auricular surface (to the sacrum), greater sciatic notch (the sciatic nerve).
  • Ischium: the posteroinferior part; the ischial tuberosity (you sit on it) and ischial spine.
  • Pubis: the anteromedial part; pubic crest, pubic rami, and the pubic symphysis in front.
  • The obturator foramen is framed by ischium and pubis; the female pelvis is wider and shallower for childbirth.

Ilium · Ischium · Pubis, fused at the acetabulum.

Part 2 · Thigh

The femur

Lower limb
The longest, strongest bone, proximal to distal
RegionMarkings
ProximalHead (into the acetabulum) with the fovea capitis (a ligament pit), neck (common fracture site), greater and lesser trochanters.
ShaftThe linea aspera, a rough ridge down the posterior shaft, a muscle attachment line.
DistalMedial and lateral condyles (to the tibia), medial and lateral epicondyles, and the intercondylar fossa between the condyles.

Part 2 · Knee and leg

Patella, tibia, and fibula

Lower limb
The kneecap and the two leg bones
BoneMarkings
PatellaThe kneecap, a sesamoid bone in the quadriceps tendon; a broad base above and a pointed apex below.
TibiaMedial, the weight-bearing shin bone: medial and lateral condyles, tibial tuberosity (patellar ligament), the sharp anterior border (the shin), and the medial malleolus.
FibulaLateral, slender, does not bear weight: a head proximally and the lateral malleolus distally.

Part 2 · Foot

The foot

Lower limb
  • Tarsals: seven ankle bones. The talus articulates with the tibia and fibula and receives the body's weight; the calcaneus is the heel, the largest tarsal. The others are the navicular, cuboid, and three cuneiforms.
  • Metatarsals: five bones of the sole, numbered 1 to 5 from the great toe.
  • Phalanges: fourteen per foot. The great toe (hallux) has two; the other toes have three each.
  • Arches: the medial and lateral longitudinal arches and the transverse arch spread body weight and spring back with each step.

Part 2 · Clinical

The skeleton that carries your weight

Clinical
  • A broken hip is usually a fracture of the femoral neck, common in older adults with osteoporosis; the head's blood supply runs close by, so the fracture can threaten the head.
  • The ASIS and iliac crest are landmarks felt through the skin, used for injection sites and pelvic alignment.
  • The tibia bears weight and the fibula does not, so a length of fibula can be taken as a bone graft; the lateral malleolus is the common ankle-fracture site.
  • When the medial longitudinal arch drops, the result is flat feet.

Part 3

Check Your Understanding

From recall to reasoning, then the takeaways and your study questions.

Part 3 · Recall (DOK 1)

Name the bone

Practice

Depth of Knowledge 1 · Recall

Which bone is the only bony link between the upper limb and the axial skeleton?

Answer

The clavicle. Its sternal end meets the manubrium at the sternoclavicular joint, the sole bony attachment of the upper limb to the axial skeleton.

Part 3 · Skill / concept (DOK 2)

Which bone, which side?

Practice

Depth of Knowledge 2 · Skill / concept

In anatomical position, which forearm bone is on the lateral (thumb) side?

Answer

The radius is lateral (thumb side). The ulna is medial and forms the main hinge of the elbow with the trochlea of the humerus.

Part 3 · Strategic reasoning (DOK 3)

Reason it through

Practice

Depth of Knowledge 3 · Strategic reasoning

Why can a length of fibula be harvested as a bone graft with little effect on standing?

Answer

The fibula bears no weight. The tibia is the weight-bearing leg bone, so removing part of the slender lateral fibula leaves standing and walking largely intact.

Wrap-up

Key takeaways

Summary
  • The appendicular skeleton = the limbs plus the girdles that attach them.
  • Upper limb: clavicle + scapula, humerus, radius + ulna, then carpals (8), metacarpals (5), phalanges (14).
  • The hip bone is ilium + ischium + pubis, fused at the acetabulum.
  • Lower limb: femur (+ patella), tibia + fibula, then tarsals (7), metatarsals (5), phalanges (14).
  • The tibia bears weight, the fibula does not; the radius is lateral, the ulna makes the elbow hinge.

Given, not Googled

Study questions

Synthesis

Answer these in writing, in your own words. Bring them to class; they are the reasoning the exams ask for.

  • Name the two bones of the pectoral girdle and how the girdle attaches the upper limb to the axial skeleton.
  • Trace the bones of the upper limb from shoulder to fingertips, naming each.
  • Name the three bones that fuse to form the hip bone and the point where all three meet.
  • Compare the tibia and fibula by which one bears weight and how you would tell them apart.
