Part 2

The Synovial Joint

The freely movable joint, built for motion with little friction.

Part 2 · Structure

Synovial joint structure

Synovial

A synovial cavity sits between the bones, so the joint is freely movable (a diarthrosis).

  • Articular cartilage: hyaline cartilage on the bone ends; reduces friction and absorbs shock but does not bind the bones.
  • Articular capsule: an outer fibrous membrane (attached to periosteum) and an inner synovial membrane (areolar tissue with elastic fibers).
  • Ligaments: thickened fibrous bundles that resist strain and prevent dislocation.
  • Synovial fluid & fat pads: fill the cavity; the fluid lubricates and the pads cushion.

Synovial fluid (hyaluronic acid) lubricates, nourishes the avascular cartilage, and clears debris.

Part 2 · Fluid & accessories

Synovial fluid and accessories

Synovial
The fluid does three jobs; accessory structures refine the joint
StructureRole
Synovial fluidLubricates (a thin film), nourishes the avascular cartilage, and defends (phagocytes clear debris). It is gel-like at rest and thins with movement, the warm-up effect.
Menisci (articular discs)Crescent fibrocartilage pads that improve fit, absorb shock, and spread fluid.
LabrumA fibrocartilage rim that deepens a ball-and-socket socket (shoulder, hip).
Extra- & intracapsular ligamentsOutside the capsule (knee collaterals) and inside it but outside the cavity (knee cruciates).
Bursae & tendon sheathsFluid sacs that reduce friction; sheaths are tubular bursae wrapping tendons in tight spaces.
SupplyThe nerves that move the joint carry pain; vessels feed the capsule, but cartilage is fed by synovial fluid.

Part 3 · Range of motion

What sets range of motion

Synovial
  • Bone shape: the structure and fit of the articulating bones.
  • Ligament tension: taut ligaments restrict and direct the bones.
  • Muscle arrangement: muscle tension reinforces the ligament restraint.
  • Contact of soft parts: one surface meeting another limits movement.
  • Hormones: relaxin loosens the pubic symphysis and pelvic ligaments late in pregnancy.
  • Disuse: an unused joint loses range as fluid, ligament flexibility, and muscle decline.

Part 4

The Shoulder, the Knee, and Disorders

The most mobile joint, the most complex joint, and how joints fail.

Part 4 · Shoulder

The shoulder (glenohumeral) joint

Joints
  • A ball-and-socket joint: the head of the humerus in the shallow glenoid cavity. The most freely movable joint, and the price of that mobility is instability.
  • It moves so freely because the capsule is loose and the glenoid is shallow relative to the large humeral head.
  • Supports: the articular capsule, coracohumeral and three glenohumeral ligaments, the transverse humeral ligament, the glenoid labrum (deepens the socket), and four bursae.
  • Most stability comes not from ligaments but from the rotator cuff (SITS): supraspinatus, infraspinatus, teres minor, subscapularis.

Part 4 · Knee

The knee (tibiofemoral) joint

Joints

A modified hinge, the largest and most complex joint, and the most often injured. Three joints share one cavity: medial and lateral tibiofemoral, plus patellofemoral.

The four ligaments at the heart of knee stability
LigamentWhat it resists
Tibial collateralForces opening the knee on the medial side.
Fibular collateralForces opening the knee on the lateral side.
Anterior cruciate (ACL)Hyperextension and the tibia sliding forward; torn in ~70% of serious knee injuries.
Posterior cruciate (PCL)The tibia sliding backward; important on stairs and inclines.
The menisci are the C-shaped medial and O-shaped lateral fibrocartilage discs. The unhappy triad: a lateral blow tears the tibial collateral, the medial meniscus, and the ACL together.

Part 5

Check Your Understanding

From recall to reasoning, then the takeaways and your study questions.

Part 5 · Recall (DOK 1)

Classify the joint

Practice

Depth of Knowledge 1 · Recall

A tooth in its socket is which type of fibrous joint?

Answer

Gomphosis. A cone-shaped peg held in a socket by the periodontal ligament; immovable, a synarthrosis. (A symphysis is cartilaginous.)

Part 5 · Skill / concept (DOK 2)

Match shape to type

Practice

Depth of Knowledge 2 · Skill / concept

The thumb's carpometacarpal joint, where each surface is both concave and convex, is which type?

Answer

Saddle. Each surface is both concave and convex, biaxial; the classic example is the thumb carpometacarpal joint.

Part 5 · Strategic reasoning (DOK 3)

Reason it through

Practice

Depth of Knowledge 3 · Strategic reasoning

Why is the shoulder the most easily dislocated major joint?

Answer

Shallow socket, loose capsule. The large humeral head sits in a shallow glenoid with a loose capsule, so it is the most mobile and the least stable; the rotator cuff, not the ligaments, holds it in place.

Wrap-up

Key takeaways

Summary
  • Classify joints by structure (fibrous, cartilaginous, synovial) and by function (syn-, amphi-, diarthrosis).
  • Fibrous: suture, syndesmosis, gomphosis, interosseous membrane. Cartilaginous: synchondrosis, symphysis.
  • A synovial joint = articular cartilage + capsule (fibrous + synovial membrane) + synovial fluid in a cavity, with ligaments, menisci, and bursae.
  • Six synovial types: plane, hinge, pivot, condyloid, saddle, ball-and-socket (uniaxial to triaxial).
  • Shoulder = mobile ball-and-socket held by the rotator cuff; knee = modified hinge held by collaterals and cruciates.
